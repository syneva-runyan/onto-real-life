Morocco was a shock, but in a life-lessons, enlightening kind of way. Very little could have prepared me for the culture shock, maze-like Medinas, lawless (but heavily policed) roads, and exotic wildlife there.

CULTURE

Islam was introduced to Morocco with an Arab invasion in 680 AD. Nearly 99% of the population practice Islam today (possibly legit source).

The call to prayer at 5am sunrises, the fact that some men weren’t allowed to shake my hand (since I ‘m a woman) were new to me. In the first city we visited, Chefchaouen , we weren’t able to get a glass of wine with dinner anywhere inside the Medina – devastating after a long day of stressful travel. (Just kidding, I wasn’t actually devastated. Or was I…)

Medinas themselves were new to me. Medinas are small, fortified, North African communities that have several of the following: Mosques, fountains, bakeries, narrow winding street markets called souks…

It’s common to have someone yell at you to get out of the way for their donkey(ies) when walking around inside the Medina.

Medinas aren’t designed with urban living as their primary focus – they cater to a religious life style, with mosques and other religious buildings playing a central role in their communities.

Of all the cultural differences, the attitude of opportunistic salesmen was the most exhausting.

We learned very quickly that EVERYTHING WAS A TRAP. This included the seemingly nice guy offering to lead up to our hostel when we were lost. He proceeded to lead us around in circles before demanding money. The traps included the friendly server at breakfast who recommended a Berber rug shop, where his friend, the owner, promised us we wouldn’t be pressured into buy things… and then sat us, gave us tea, and pressured us into buying things. Even the ‘official’ tour guide recommended to us in Fez told us very little of the city’s history, focusing more of our attention toward the artisan good shops, where we were pressured into buying things. We learned how to say “La shukraan” (no thankyou) before the end of the first day in Morroco, and as the week progressed, our las became more and more forceful.


CITIES

Once recovering from the initial culture shock (and upgrading our level of la shurkaans) I started to enjoy more of the souks.

I was able to appreciate the subtle (or not so subtle) differences between Chefchaouen, Fez, and Marrakesh.

Fez

Fez felt the most “authentic”. The Medina’s streets were half the width of those in Marrakesh. There were far fewer tourists. The whole city has been declared a world heritage site, and the white walls and cultural buildings were impressively well maintained. In the past few years, the US and other UN countries have been contributing money towards the restoration of certain areas. Specifically, a canal that runs through the heart of the Media is being rebuilt. I’d love to come back in a few years and see the empty waterway gushing with water and the scaffolding cleared from the beautiful building facades. At the same time, I wonder how much the city will be changed by the expected influx of tourists. Will the authentic Arabic culture become more westernized, more similar to how Marrakesh has developed? The people I met in Fez seem to cherish their culture heavily, but at the same time,

95% of the city works in artisan trades.

The success of these businesses are tied closely to the success of tourism. Will this dependency on tourism dictate changes in culture?

Chefchaouen

Chefchaouen is also heavily dependent on the tourism industry. The whole city is white-washed in blue for religious reasons, and, I suspect, to continue attracting tourists. We walked around the streets taking

pictures, checking out the market stalls, and being offered hash at various points by the locals. We only spent a day in the city, but right before leaving, we walked in the ruins of an old estate in the middle of the city. It was easy to picture how ornate the area used to be, and the views from the tower were breath taking.

Marrakesh

Marrakesh was the most Western of the cities we visited. It’s further south than both Chefchaouen and Fez, and has more Berber influence than Arabic in comparison to the northern cities. (Berbers being native nomads of the Sahara dessert) There is even a European market in Marrakesh, where all of the artisan shops have fixed prices, no haggling needed or allowed.

Not to disappoint, there were still plenty of opportunities to be ripped off or misguided by shop keepers and street workers. At one point I noticed a crowd gathered around slithering cobras. I was perfectly ready to donate money in exchange for a picture. What I wasn’t ready for was the guy who walked up to me, put a snake around my neck, tried to take my camera from me for a picture. He refused to take the snake off my neck when I said no. I paid him money to leave me alone. The picture of the cobra isn’t even that great.

WILDLIFE

Other wild life of Morocco contribute to successful businesses as well. Wild monkeys, camels, and tree goats were some of my favorite parts of the trip.

On our drive south from Fez, we stopped by a park with the hope of seeing the monkeys. After a good amount of time searching, we regretfully turned back. A dropped pair of brand name eye glasses in the woods did offer circumstantial evidence that the monkeys existed and were mischievous however. Sulking in the car, I silently and mournfully reconciled with the fact that I wouldn’t be able to pet baby monkeys – but I gave up hope too soon!

Fifteen minutes down the road, we saw a family of monkeys chilling on the side of the road! Us and a handful of other cars stopped to take pictures.

There were men selling peanuts to give the monkeys, but for ethical reasons, we decided not to feed them.

Tree goats are another road side attraction in Morocco. On the road from Marrakesh to Essouria, there are fields full of argan tress and goats. The goats sometimes eat the nuts that grown on these trees, not letting the inconvenience of skinny branches or height get to them. I read online that sometimes tree goat scene are staged.

Farmers will take their goats and tie their legs in tree specifically to attract tourists.

Because of this, when we saw the tree goats with the suspicious looking farmers sitting near by, we opted not to stop the car for photos. We did a slow drive by, getting the following sketchy-looking picture. Thankfully, there were no ropes in sight. We therefor deemed these ”natural” tree goats.

Most famous among all the animals in Morocco is the Camel. At sunset in Palm Grove outside of Marrakesh, we took an hour long camel ride. The pictures speak for themselves here. If we had more time, I’d loved to ride the camels in the dessert, across the sand dunes of the Sahara. That said – in the short ride I did take – my stomach did feel unsettled and I had light motion sickness. Any longer on the camel could have been a bad idea for me.

DRIVING

The car we rented enabled us to see fifty times more of Morocco in a week than taking trains or buses would have. Because we had a car, we got to see monkeys & checkout random rest stops with beautiful views. On the other hand, a guide in Marrakesh said that

People in Morocco “don’t drive. They avoid each other.”

That statement is accurate. There’s a cross walk 5 feet away – why unnecessarily waste the energy to cross there?

We could have ran over children in the cities so many times. In the country, cars would pass on winding country roads, speeding past with little regard for safety or speed limits. There were also a lot of cop checkpoints in the country. Despite safe driving, we were ticketed twice, once for speeding and once for passing a tediously slow bus. Tickets had to be paid on the spot with cash. We didn’t ask what would have happened if we hadn’t had cash on us. Given paradoxical lack of law abiding drivers and multitude of cops, I have no idea how people in Morocco aren’t ticketed multiple times on a daily basis.

Altogether

Morocco was an amazing country to visit. I’m glad I went with relatively little expectations. This allowed me to soak up the culture and experiences without much preconceived bias. There is so much I wasn’t able to fit into the week that I was there that I know I’ll have to go back for one day. Until then, I continuing spreading conspiracy theories about tree goats, fondly go back through my photos every once in awhile, and maybe start coming up with a real travel plan for my second visit 😊


OTHER NOTES:

Tannery: Fez is known as the “capital of leather.” We stopped at one on our tour. Entrance came with free “gas mask” for the smell (fresh mint leaves)

Mint Tea – Fresh Mint tea is served with most meals and offered by salesmen in most shops.

Squatting toilets – Would encourage packing your own toilet paper and hand sanitizer if traveling around Morocco

Ifran – A random city in Morocco designed to look and feel like a Swiss mountain village. Reportedly one of the cleanest cities in the world. Refreshing to stop through on a drive south from Fez, near the monkeys.

Essouria – Pretty seaside city. Markets here were more reasonably priced, and the Medina held it’s own charm. Game of Thrones shooting location.

Dinner at the Governor’s – Sometimes the best experiences you have traveling are completely unplanned. I met up with my sister in Oarzazate, who was tagging along with a friend on a school trip, which was organized by the governor’s son. Very generous and friendly man and family.
