There is So. Much. To. Do. on the south island, where do I even start?

I've probably listened to the song "99 Red Balloons" more than once for every balloon in the song.

And why, you ask, have I listened to that song So. Many. Times? There is So. Much. Driving to do on the South Island (and I have a severely lacking music library).

Everything is spread out, and there's the added inconvenience of mountains everywhere.

Partly due of this distance between things, and partly due to the sheer amount of things to do, the three weeks I spent on the south island were not enough. six weeks would have been more appropriate.

There is that whole winter thing though... So I'm headed off to somewhere warmer next week (Indonesia, whoopp), and'll plan on returning when it's warmer and camping/hiking is possible without the constant risk of turning into an ice cube.

From the peanut butter factory, to climbing on a glacier, to hating myself for hiking two and a half hours straight uphill - what I did fit in into the last three weeks has been nothing short of phenomenal. Rather than writing an exhaustive narrative of everything, I've compiled a top-8 list. It's made up of a mixture of hiking, outdoor activities, random places, and events. Even though they may seem unrelated, all of them epitomize quintessential aspects of the Southern kiwi experience.

1. Waking up in Wanaka

Am I too young to retire?

I could happily live out the end of my days admiring the mountains surrounding the serene lake front and taking short (or long) day hikes every morning in Wanaka.

The hike to Roy's Peak here is instagram-famous. When I read the DOC's (Department of Conservation's) description of the track as "demanding," with a time estimate of 5-6 hours, I scoffed. Psh... The estimates are always overdone, and from the car park at the peak's base, the "mount" looks more like a large hill.

Holy crap I was wrong.

There are no downhill breaks, no meandering flat sections on the hike up. No exaggeration, the hiking trail goes uphill for two and a half hours straight.

A lot of people make it the two hours up to the picture viewpoint then turn around. Doing the extra 30 minutes up to the peak is well worth the effort though.

I claim bragging rights for making it all the way up.


2. The Peanut Butter Factory (Nelson)

You'd run out of numbers before adding up the number of peanut butter sandwiches I ate as a child.

Naturally, as soon as I realized peanut butter factories existed and that there was one in Nelson that gave tours, I had to go. This wasn't an option, or a "if I have time," or "if I feel like it" thing. Had. To. Go.

The tour included peanut butter tasting, a make-your-own-peanut-butter segment, and a brief history of the founder, who used to own laundry mats, then started going blind at 55 (from mascular degeneration) and needed a new profession.

While I do love peanut butter and 100% loved the tour, I didn't pick Pic's for my top list for being amazing in itself. It's representative of a number of small, passionate, community focused businesses I came across on the South island.

Pic's does things like donate free peanut butter for school lunches across Nelson. When the hand-automated process of filling peanut butter jars was replaced with machines, employees were promoted or re-assigned instead of fired.

Leaning about the jet boat operators' "locals' day" in Queenstown raising money for youths, and the community support in response to a parasite affecting oyster farms in Steward Island, and the hostel owners who trade off vacation time in Hanmer Springs impressed me while traveling throughout the South Island.

Kiwis support each other and their communities, which is beautiful and something places all over the world could learn from.


3. Jumping off a Bridge (Queenstown)

If you love extreme outdoor adventuring, you need to go to Queenstown. Like, yesterday. So hop on a plane right now.

Since I'm trying not to go broke, I wasn't able to go paragliding and jet boating and canyon swinging, AND skydiving... but I did at least go Bungy jumping in Queenstown.

After an interesting history of fed-up wives and evil spirits, modern Bungy jumping was reportedly invented outside of Queenstown during the 80s.

I jumped off the Kawarau Bridge, one of the first public Bungy sites in the World. I love heights, but there was a little voice screaming in the back of my head when I stepped off the bridge (ok, so it wasn't in my head, I actually screamed), because I really didn't want to die.

4. Stewart & Ulva Islands

I didn't even know they had parrots in New Zealand, so when I heard something rustling in the trees above me, the last thing I expected to see was not just one parrot, but a family of parrots.

Outside of Dunedin, people pay $100+ nzd to see albatross in an exhibit (Albatrosses? What is the plural of albatross?). On a ferry to Ulva (small wildlife reserve island) from Stewart Island, an albatross took interest in out boat and landed in the water right off the port side.(Both me and the professional photographer I was chatting with had our lens caps on...)

Even as someone who knows very little about birds, I loved being on Stewart Island and discovering a wide array of wildlife on the beautiful coastal and bush hikes.

5. Ice Explorer Tour (Franz Joseph)

"Ice Explorer" set false expectations for the tour I did on Franz Joseph glacier, since the group moved at a pace of ~less than 0.5mph. Nevertheless, getting to fly on a helicopter, wearing crampons for the first time, and shuffling through ice tunnel was something I won't forget.

Because of the recession of the Franz Joseph and Fox glaciers in the past few years (thanks Donald Trump and global warming), the only way to access the glaciers is via helicopter.

Helicopter flights are expensive, which makes glacier tours expensive. Ontop of the expense, the weather around Franz Joseph and the Fox Glaciers are so variable, that there's a good chance on any given day that helicopters won't be able to land, either canceling your trip, or stranding you on the glacier overnight.

I wasn't stranded, but my first trip was canceled. Our group made it as far as zipping up our tour jackets before they canceled. I was lucky enough to reschedule for 8am the next morning, but a lot of people weren't hanging around and had to be refunded.

7. Mt. Cook Hiking

Thank you Julia for this recommendation. The area around Mt. Cook is full of beautiful hiking. The Hooker Valley track specifically has multiple suspension bridges, cloudy blue streams, and a partially frozen lake. There are some benefits to traveling in winter.



8. Roadside Surprises

The roadsides on the South Island were the perfect manifestation of kiwi humor & eccentricity.

It's very possible to get around the country using buses, but if you do have a car, make sure to pull over at the random roadside attractions. You're guaranteed to be stunned by either beauty or positive ridiculousness.

I've loved visiting both the North and South Islands in New Zealand, but of the two, the South's unique culture and unreal beauty epitomized in my top 8 picks was the one that captured my heart. The only thing I really disliked on the South island was the lack of big cities (and cheaper pints). Size is relative though, and I hear the country is recruiting developers. Maybe I'll be back again with more time to do everything I've yet to do. Maybe on that trip I'll even bring better (and more) music.

Other Highlights

  • Lake Matheson: Pictures don't do this place justice. Go at sunrise for the best views. Keep in mind that once you park it's still a 20 minute walk to the main viewpoint. Don't do what I did, wake up early and aim to get to the carpark at sunrise...
  • Abel Tasman Kayak Tour: Seal pups are just like puppies - curious and playful. One literally swam to the bow of my double kayak to say hi.
  • Milford Sound: A lot of people I talked to called Milford Sound the highlight of their trip. The fjord was magnificent and the waterfalls majestic. If I come back to the South Island, I plan on doing overnight hiking in this area.
  • Dunedin Street Art: The isite gives out maps for a great street art crawl around the city center.
  • Queenstown Winterfest: Smaller-scaled than I expected, but had some good laughs at the dog barking competition and several fun nights out. Queenstown is a tiny city, but somehow they've managed to fit 42 bars inside the city limits. That says something right there about what to expect from Queenstown.
  • Lake Tekapo Astonomy Tour: My tour was canceled due to bad weather, but completely different types of people in completely different circumstances independently talked about how amazing this tour was.
