I did relatively little preparation for this trip. I didn’t even plan on going to Malta really. I wanted to go to Greece. When I looked up flights though, Malta returns were only Â£100. Annndddd…. flight booked.

My expectation for Malta – chilling in the Mediterranean sunshine on white sandy beaches. In actuality – I spent 20 minutes on a beach. That was it. The rest of the time I spent in accidental-exploration, snapping pictures every 10 seconds and soaking up fantastic culture and history. And a lot of sun. Enough to get burnt at least once a day.

Touristing started at the Blue Grotto – a bioluminescent cave on the southwest of the island. I showed up 20 minutes before the first boat tours. Sounds extremely unlike me to be early for anything, right? Be proud!

Actually, don’t be proud, because really, I just didn’t think of looking up tour times. I showed up and just expected to get on a boat. Opps.

When the tours did start, a guy sitting in the back of my boat looked exactly like Jake Ballard from Scandal, and I think the guy sitting next to me said he had spent a year in St. Louis as a circus instructor. I’m awful with accents though, so who knows. I didn’t ask him to clarify. What if he was actually a science teacher or circuits instructor? I’d much rather have that guy live in my memory dressed in a neon green acrobatics suit, jumping through hoops over lions dens and landing in a handstand on top of an elephant.

The bioluminescent that morning was weak, but the caves were striking none-the-lesss.

After the boat tour, my plan was to bus over to Marsaxlokk, a nearby, sleepy fishing village.

The bus ride started well. I got off at a stop, hopped onto the next route and…. 20 minutes later, I realised the stops we were passing were not, in fact, listed on my google maps route. Even though I had questioned the bus driver, the bus I was on was traveling the wrong way. This is why I have trust issues -_-

Over an hour away from Marsaxlokk at the point, I improvised, cutting the town out of my plans, and heading straight into the three cities for lunch.

Moment of silence here for the never-taken Marsaxlokk instagram shots.

The “Three Cities” of Malta are made up of Victorriosa, Senglea, and Cospicui. They epitomized the rich history and old-city beauty of the country. I hopped off the bus in Cospicui however, and I realised I had no plan of what actually to do in the three cities… I walked on the water front a bit, and saw some signs pointing to a war museum. Google reviews looked decent, so I dropped by.

The museum turned out to be a really good decision. A) I learned a lot B) The bottom floor of the museum was open WWII air raid tunnel you could walk around and get lost in. The small spaces, earthy smell, and dim lighting complimented what must have been the constant state of stress and fear on the island decades ago during War.

After museuming, I continued my waterfront walking, visited a fort at the end of the three cities reach, and took a brief detour to Vallenta (probably the most touristy part of the island I went to) where I intended to stop by a beautiful Cathedral from the Order of St. John’s time in Malta.

For anyone who doesn’t know (I had no idea), the Order of St. John was a military-religious group formed during the time of the crusades. Malta, because of it’s location between Europe (Italy) and Africa, is a desirable location for any political power wanting to exhibit dominance in the Mediterranean. The Order of St. John’s, possibly on orders from the Vatican(?) crushed the Ottoman Turk’s attempt to rule Malta in the 1500s and ran the country for 2.5 centuries. From there, the French had a brief but prosperous reign, and then the English took over, like they always do everywhere. All three cultures have left their own influences on the island.

I never went made it into the cathedral in Vallenta. The front doors were closed, and I was so sunburned and dehydrated at this point, that I didn’t feel like finding another entrance – if there even was one – or looking up opening times. Instead, I wandered around in search of a pharmacy, stumbling onto Victoria’s Gate and some beautiful doorways.

Sunscreen freshly applied, I hoped on another bus to Popeye’s Village. The Village is a the former set location for Popeye’s TV show now open as a tourist attraction. Disclaimer – I’ve never seen the show. I saw the set on instagram, and it looked beautiful, so I went.

The village was a series of houses converted into museums. Actors roamed around, there was a minigolf course, a free shot bar with some way-too-sweet rum, and (on nice days) visitors get a short boat tour in the bay. I showed up just a bit before closing time, so didn’t get to properly enjoy the place, but would definitely go again.

The next day of my trip I spent in Gozo. The morning was miserable. It was rainy, I was cold, and an obnoxious group of school children had kept me up until 2am and woke me up at 6:30 at my hotel. Worst. Behaved. Kids. Ever.

I had pinned 3 spots on google maps to visit. Each of the places were busable – but at the ferry, there were people shoving hop-on-hop-off tour bus brochures in my face. Usually, I hate hop on hop off tours. The commentary is poorly done, the stops mediocre, and the bus-riders suppppeeerrr touristy. The discounted 10 euro ticket equated to about the same amount I was going to spend on the bus rides mapped though. If the buses in Gozo were anything like the ones in Malta, they were super crowded, inconsistent with timing, and “rickety” would be a good word to describe their physicality. I bought a bus ticket, which ended up being an awesome call.

The first planned stopped was Cittadella, a fortified city and world heritage site. As I got off the bus, the rain let up. I enjoyed a basically empty tour around the “city.” I use city with quotes, because only two families currently live within the walls, there are a small handful of shops, and a church inside the city walls. That’s it.

One of my favorite parts of Cittadella was the visitor museum, which advertised a bluetooth-navigation tour map. Estimated download time on the wifi there though was 40 minutes. I wasn’t that committed to trying out the experience.

 

 

 

 

 

Any who has ever seen a tour guide for the Mediterranean, ever in passing at a book store would recognize my next stop – The azure window in Dwejra. Unfortunately, the window feel down several weeks ago, a few days after I bought my plane ticket. The area itself however is still full of natural beauty. It’s there that most of the limestone used for buildings in Gozo is collected.

From here the bus tour brought me to a random, beautiful church, and then to the Gigantia temple.

The Gigantia temple I was really excited for – It’s an open-air stone temple that’s been around since ~3200 BC. Like Stonehenge, scholars have no idea how the people who built the temple had the technology required for the construction. My theory is – since we keep finding archaeological items and places that people were clearly not advanced enough to construct – People way back when were actually advanced enough to construct them. Imaging these people as intelligent makes them more relatable than the cave-man images I’ve always pictured in my head.

With the three places I had wanted to go in Gozo marked off, I would have headed back to Malta if not for the tour bus. It turns out that Xaghra, a beach right around the corner from the temple, is overlooked by Calypso’s Cave. In Homer’s Odyssey, this is where Odysseus stopped off for SEVEN YEARS on his way back home, spending time with Calypso in her cave. His poor wife.

I didn’t have enough time to hike up to the cave, but here was where I spent the exclusive 20 minutes of beach time on my trip.

The next day, rather than traditional touristing, I tried something new. Scuba Diving! Won’t go into too much detail here, but looking back, I think I enjoyed it? At the time, I was annoyed with the weights I had to wear and annoyed with myself for only bringing a bikini to dive it. I’m sure I looked ridiculous. And I was cold! I properly impressed the scuba instructor by the end of the lesson though. He told me I was a “natural” and it would be a shame if I didn’t keep up with it. I didn’t mention to him that the only reason I had kept up with him so well under water was that I got a bunch of water up my nose when trying to clear my googles at the beginning of the swim. Rather than clearing the condensation out of them for the rest of the time, I just stayed as close to him as I could. With limited sight, heck no was I going to let myself loose him.

The lesson ended sooner than I expected, so I headed to Rabat for the afternoon, one of the older cities on the island.

Once getting there, I realised I had zero desire to museum. Instead, after a lunch and some walking around, I headed towards the Victoria lines. My phone said it was a 50 minute walk or 45 minutes bus ride. I opted to walked. That was a mistake. People don’t know how to drive, and turns out there aren’t many side walks outside city centres.

When I finally made it to the line however, I wasn’t disappointed. The line is a natural fault that runs across the island, which historic settlers took advantage of to build up defensive walls. There were also a handful of caves carved into the section of the wall I started at. I would have loved to go in and explore, but the trail was very poorly maintained, and recently I’ve been listening to a lot of murder-focused podcasts, reading a lot of Scandinavian noir, watching a lot of crime focused TV – all in all, I’m properly sketched out by any vaguely unsafe situation. Vaguely unsafe situations include unmaintained caves on a remote island with possible signs of homeless inhabitants. Maybe next time I’ll bring one of my friends who work out daily and constantly chug protein shakes. Or just one of my brothers, that describes them all pretty well.

While hiking along the line, I felt like a queen looking down upon my island kingdom.

Surprisingly, I only ran into two people over the 2 hour time period I hiked along it. I’m not sure if it’s that the line isn’t well know, or if there are recent murders that happened in one of the sketch patches, so tourists are now discouraged from walking along it alone.

In total, the line covers 12km across the island. I only walked a small portion, but next time, I’d love to spend a whole day cross the trails, ending on the west side right in time for a sunset.

All in all, I had an amazing time on Malta. Four days weren’t enough to do everything I wanted, but that just means I have an excuse to go back. Despite landing with relatively few plans and completely incorrect exceptions, just rolling with it resulted in an amazing long weekend.

PS: OTHER NOTES FROM THE TRIP:

  • While the public transportation system was fairly well ran on the island, renting a car would have given me much more freedom. Would definitely recommend getting one for shorter trips.
  • I stayed at the San Anton hotel, and it was highly rated on hostel world. Would not recommend. The room was dirty, the walls paper thing, the management unsympathetic to the fact kids were literally screaming in the hallways all night. Hotels there were inexpensive, so I could have stayed at a much nicer place.
  • The Blue Lagoon and Camino islands are still on my list of things to do in Malta and Gozo. Boat service ended at 6:30 pm though, so if you want to catch a sunset there, you either have to handle your own boat or stay at the island’s one hotel. Would do if returning.
